Magma reservoir dynamics

Introduction

Magmatic reservoirs located in the upper crust have been shown to result from the repeated intrusions of new magmas, and spend much of the time as a crystal-rich mush (Fig. 1). A mush is a magma which has cooled and crystallized and where crystals are touching together to form a rigid skeleton inhibiting its ability to flow easily. The geometry of the intruded batch of new magmas and its interaction with the surrounding mush may greatly affect the thermal and compositional evolution of the reservoir. Despite advances in our understanding of the physical processes that may occur in a magmatic reservoir, the resulting architecture and dynamics of the composite system remains poorly constrained.

Main goals are:

  • Determine the geometry of magma intrusions in mush and what are the controlling physical parameters
  • Explore what dynamics may favor efficient mingling and/or mixing between the intrusion and host mush

Collaborators: Alain Burgisser (CNRS, University Savoi Mont-Blanc, Chambery, France) and George Bergantz (University of Washington, Seattle, USA)

Method

We perform numerical simulations using a Discrete Element Model coupled with a Computational Fluid Dynamic solver (CFD-DEM) to explore the dynamics existing in the magmatic reservoir during and after the emplacement of crystal free magmas within the crystal rich mush. The model is based on the open source code MFIX (Multiphase Flow with Interphases eXchanges) developed at the NETL (National Energy Technology Laboratory).

The discrete element method considers explicitly the crystals a discrete entities (here spheres or disks; Fig. 2), which can interact togethers through frictional contacts or other physical processes (i.e. heat transfer). Basically, for each particle and time step, the model computes the acceleration of the particle from the sum of the forces applied to it using the Newtonâ€™s second law before updating its velocity and position.

The motion of the fluid is computed on a regular grid (Fig. 2) using the iterative solver SIMPLE (Patankar, 1980), which uses a finite volume method. The two phases exchange momentum (drag and pressure forces) and heat such that the dynamics of one phase affect tho one of the other phase (Fig. 2).

Results

We explored the geometry and emplacement dynamics of a new magma within a magmatic mush. Simulations consist in the injection of a crystal free magma inside a crystal rich layer (Fig. 3). We performed several runs by varying the physical properties of injected melt (density and dynamic viscosity) and the injection velocity.

Simulations were sorted in three regimes: mixing bowl (Anim. 1), lateral spreading (Anim. 2), and rising (Anim. 3) regimes. The distribution of the different regimes against dimensionless numbers (Atwood number and dimensionless viscosity contrast) showed that the dynamics of the emplacement of new magma in the reservoirs was first order controlled by the density contrast existing between the melt phases of the host and intruded materials.

Figure 3: Simulations initial conditions. The blue and green background colors correspond to the host and intruded melt phases, respectively.

Figure 4: Distribution of the runs as a function of the Atwood number and the dimensionless viscosity contrast. On the abscissa, the black and red coordinates indicate the Atwood numbers computed with the melt phase densities and with the bulk densities, respectively. Each square corresponds to a run and its color depends on the observed regime (blue: rising; black: fluidization; red: lateral spreading). All runs displayed on this figure were performed with the same dimensionless injection velocity.

Results differ from the hypothesis commonly used in volcanology that the bulk density (averaged density taking into account the liquid, solid and gas phases and their respective concentrations) contrasts control the motion of mass in magmatic reservoirs. The viscosity contrast between the two melt phases and injection velocity were found to have a lesser importance on the emplacement of new magma in the reservoir. All simulations showed the initial radial growth of the intruded batch above the inlet before the main dynamics set up. Higher injection rate lengthen the duration between the beginning of the injection and set up of the main dynamics, and in turn increase the volume of the initial batch.
