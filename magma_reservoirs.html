Magma reservoir dynamics

Objectives

Magmatic reservoirs located in the upper crust have been shown to result from the repeated intrusions of new magmas, and spend much of the time as a crystal-rich mush. A mush is a magma that have cooled and crystallized where crystals are touching together to form a rigid skeleton inhibiting its ability to easily flow. The geometry of the intrusion of new magmas may greatly affect the thermal and compositional evolution of the reservoir. Despite advances in our understanding of the physical processes that may occur in a magmatic reservoir, the resulting architecture and dynamics of the composite system remains poorly constrained.

Main objectives are:

-Determine the geometry of magma intrusions in mush and controlling physical parameters

-Explore what dynamics may lead to mingling and/or mixing between the intrusion and host mush

Collaborators: Alain Burgisser (CNRS, University Savoi Mont-Blanc, Chambery, France) and George Bergantz (University of Washington, Seattle, USA)

Method

We use numerical simulations performed with a Discrete Element Model coupled with a Computational Fluid Dynamic solver (CFD-DEM) to the dynamics during and efter the emplacement of crystal free magmas in crystal rich mush. Our model is based on the open source code MFIX develloped at the NETL.

The discrete element method considers tha crystals a discrete entities (currently sphere) that can interact together and with the surounding fluid. CFD-DEM is a powerfull method...

Past work

We explored the geometry and emplacement dynamics of new magma within magmatic mush. Here, CFD-DEM simulations consist in the injection of a crystal free magma inside a crystal rich layer (Fig. ?). We perfomred a series of experiments by varying the physical properties of injected melt (density and viscosity) and the injection velocity.

Simulations were sorted in three regimes: Mixing bowl regime (Anim. 1); Lateral spreading (Anim. 2); Rising regime (Anim. 3). Analysis of the simulation observed regimes showed that the dynamics of the emplacement of new magma in the resrrvoirs was first order controled by the density contrast existing between the howt and intruded melt phases.

Results are in contrast with the hypothesis previously used in volcanology that the bulk density (weighted average density taking into account the liquid, solid and gas phases and their respective concentrations) contrasts control the motion of mass in the magmatic reservoir. The viscosity contrast between the two melt phases and injection velocity were found to have a lesser importance on the emplacement of new magma in the reservoir
