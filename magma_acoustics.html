Introduction

The detection of melt-dominated volumes and the estimation of their physical properties are of paramount importance to enhance our ability to predict eruptive styles and to best assess volcanic hazards. The presence of eruptible magma is expected to cause the sharp decrease of the compressional wave velocity and to suppress shear waves (Caricchi et al., 2008). Tomographic images of seismic wave velocities and attenuations show the presence of partially molten rocks and crystal mush, but they have never evidenced the presence of melt-dominated bodies (e.g. Paulatto et al., 2012; Fig. 1). A comprehensive interpretation of the results of seismic velocities and attenuation tomography requires a good knowledge of the acoustic properties of the materials constituting the magmatic system.

Main goals are:

  • Develop an analytical model to estimate the acoustic properties of magmas
  • Determine what mechanism dominates the intrinsic attenuation in magmas

Method

We consider an infinite suspension of spheres in a viscous fluid in which a plane acoustic compresionnal wave is propagating. Particles are organized regularly in space, forming a 3D lattice of face-centered cubic structure (Fig. 2), in which all particles are separated by the same distance.

The propagation of an acoustic perturbation in a suspension may be described with the continuum conservation equations of both the melt and the crystal phases. To compute the acoustic properties of magmas we adapted and used the coupled phase theory, which consists in linearizing the conservative equations of each phase and imposing the oscillation of parameters affected by the propagation of the acoustic wave (e.g. velocities and densities of the phases) using complex exponentials. The exponential terms include the complex wavenumber, which contains the wave velocity in its real part and intrinsic attenuation coefficient in its imaginary part. The method consists in determining the complex wavenumber for which all the equalities in the conservative equations are verified (see Harker and Temple, 1988).

In order to apply the model to magmas, we considered three magmas representative of the different compositions that may be encountered in arc magmatic systems (basalt, andesite, and dacite). We simulated the isobaric cooling and crystallization of magmas using the MELTS model (Ghiorso, 2004) and estimated the physical properties of their constituents.

Results

We used the simulations of the cooling and crystallizations of the basalt, andesite, and dacite, to compute the compressional wave velocities using the coupled phase theory. The dacitic and andesitic magmas have similar compressional wave velocities at a given temperature, whereas the basalt has higher velocities (Fig. 3A). When the compressional wave velocities in the magmas are compared as a function of the respective crystal contents, the three curves merge (Fig. 3B). The chemical composition of a magma seems to have no effect on wave velocity other that controlling the temperature at which a given crystallinity is reached.
